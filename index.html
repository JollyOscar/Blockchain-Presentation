Real‑World Adoption

Blockchain shows up most where shared truth reduces delays, paperwork, and the need for middlemen.

Banking & Financial Services

  • International transfers can drop from days to minutes.
  • Reduces reliance on middlemen banks for transaction verification.
  • Often available 24/7 instead of business-hours-only systems.

Government / Public Sector

  • Sweden uses blockchain for land registry, replacing a slow, paper-heavy process.
  • Helps reduce manual verification and paperwork.
  • Estimated savings: €100M (~$162M CAD) annually.
Why this adoption matters

When multiple organizations must agree on the same record, blockchain can reduce delays by providing a shared, auditable ledger. That’s why it shows up in payments and public records.

Pros & Cons Today

Pros (The advantages)

  • No single point of failure: data lives on many computers, so one crash won't bring it down.
  • No middleman needed: the code and network enforce the rules, so you don't need to trust a bank or company.
  • Can't fake the history: once something's recorded, it's really hard to change or delete.
  • Open and transparent: everyone can see the same records; public chains let anyone join.
  • Cheaper and faster: cuts out middlemen fees and speeds up things like international payments.

+ many more benefits depending on use case

Cons (The disadvantages)

  • Speed limits: can't handle as many transactions as regular databases; busy times = higher fees.
  • Slower confirmations: checking with lots of computers takes longer than one central server.
  • Energy hog: some networks (like Bitcoin) use a ton of electricity.
  • Legal gray areas: rules change country to country and can shift overnight.
  • Scammer magnet: semi-anonymous nature attracts fraud and shady deals.
  • Doesn't play nice: different blockchains struggle to work together; lots of hype-driven projects fail.
Reality check: blockchain shines when multiple organizations need to agree on the same data—otherwise just use a regular database.
Slide 4

Our Verdict: YES

Why it still matters

  • It's useful when many organizations need to agree on the same data without endless back-and-forth.
  • In 2026, it's the backbone for agreed-on records in payments, supply chains, and government systems.
  • Most real wins use semi-private setups that mix trust, speed, privacy, and following the rules.

One-line summary

"Blockchain is useful when it turns getting everyone to agree from a weekly headache into automatic record-keeping."

(Not for everything—but great when lots of organizations need to coordinate.)

Slide 5

The “Surprising” Insight

2026

The Biggest Win? Getting Everyone on the Same Page

In real use (like payments and public records), blockchain's best trick isn't the hype—it's cutting down the time and hassle of getting different organizations to agree on what happened.

Why it matters
Less paperwork, fewer arguments, faster deals when everyone's looking at the same ledger.
What's surprising
Most real business use is semi-private—giving up some openness to get speed, privacy, and stay legal.
The trade‑off
Still hits limits with speed, different chains not talking, and legal stuff—works best for specific high-value problems.
