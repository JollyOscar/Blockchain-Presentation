Real‑World Adoption

Blockchain shows up most where shared truth reduces delays, paperwork, and the need for middlemen.

Banking & Financial Services

  • International transfers can drop from days to minutes.
  • Reduces reliance on middlemen banks for transaction verification.
  • Often available 24/7 instead of business-hours-only systems.

Government / Public Sector

  • Sweden uses blockchain for land registry, replacing a slow, paper-heavy process.
  • Helps reduce manual verification and paperwork.
  • Estimated savings: €100M (~$162M CAD) annually.
Why this adoption matters

When multiple organizations must agree on the same record, blockchain can reduce delays by providing a shared, auditable ledger. That’s why it shows up in payments and public records.

Pros & Cons Today

Pros (The advantages)

  • No single point of failure: data is distributed across many nodes, improving resilience.
  • Trustless transactions: rules are enforced by code + consensus, reducing reliance on intermediaries.
  • Tamper‑proof audit trail: records are hard to alter once confirmed (immutability).
  • Transparency & access: shared ledgers can improve accountability; public chains are open to anyone.
  • Efficiency: can lower fees and speed up specific workflows (e.g., cross‑border settlement).

Cons (The disadvantages)

  • Scalability bottlenecks: limited throughput compared to centralized systems; congestion raises fees.
  • Slow finality: verification across many nodes can be slower than traditional databases.
  • Energy use: Proof‑of‑Work networks can consume significant electricity.
  • Regulatory uncertainty: rules differ by country and can change quickly, adding business risk.
  • Illicit use: pseudonymity can attract scams and illegal trading, hurting adoption.
  • Interoperability & maturity: chains don’t always “talk” easily; hype can lead to misused projects.
Reality check: blockchain works best when multiple parties need a shared record—otherwise a normal database is often simpler.
Slide 4

Our Verdict: YES

Why it still matters

  • It’s relevant wherever multiple parties need the same record without constant reconciliation.
  • In 2026, it’s “infrastructure” for shared truth in payments, supply chains, and public records.
  • The best results often come from permissioned/hybrid designs that balance trust, speed, privacy, and compliance.

One-line summary

“Blockchain is valuable when it turns multi‑organization agreement from a weekly headache into an automated audit trail.”

(Not a fit for everything—but a strong fit for high‑value workflows with many stakeholders.)

Slide 5

The “Surprising” Insight

2026

The Biggest Win Is… Reconciliation

In real deployments (like payments and public records), blockchain’s “killer feature” isn’t hype—it’s reducing the time and cost of getting organizations to agree on the same data.

Why it matters
Less paperwork, fewer disputes, and faster settlement when multiple parties share a ledger.
What’s surprising
Most “serious” use is permissioned/hybrid—trading some decentralization for speed, privacy, and compliance.
The trade‑off
You still face scalability, interoperability, and regulation—so the best fit is narrow, high‑value workflows.
